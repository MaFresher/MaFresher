GENDER BASED VIOLENCE(GBV)





The most common form of GBV is intimate relationship violence. Physical, sexual, and emotional abuse, as well as controlling behaviours by a current or past intimate partner or spouse, fall under this category. Intimate partner violence can occur in heterosexual as well as same-sex relationships.We know that GBV levels are high, but reliable figures aren't always available due to a variety of circumstances, such as under-reporting of incidents. In comparison to other countries, South Africa has an extremely high rate of GBV. One in every five South African women over the age of 18 has been physically abused. Thousands of women and children are psychologically traumatized as a result of GBV, and their lives are ruined. Intimate partner violence and non-partner sexual violence are the leading drivers, according to published statistics. Gender-based violence pervades society's systems.

How can we end gender-based violence?

In the fight against GBV, we've made some progress. We've gotten a lot better at defining and comprehending it. We've improved our ability to gather data and evidence to identify and support effective solutions. We've witnessed an improvement in service awareness and accessibility. Despite progress, gender-based violence remains a problem. So, how can we stop GBV by properly addressing underlying inequalities? Recognizing that women's rights are human rights is a wonderful place to start. South Africa has a robust legal framework in place and is a signatory to several international GBV accords. Examples include the Domestic Violence Act (DVA), the Sexual Offences Act, and the Prevention and Combatting of Trafficking in Human Persons (2013) Act”. We urge South Africa’s government to ratify the ILO Convention 190, which addresses violence, discrimination and harassment in the world of work.
